# Maurice Blackburn Tone of Voice

Source: MB's official **"Brand guidelines Version 1.1"** writing guide (internal PDF, "Writing for our brand" + Appendix sections), read in full 2026-07-11. This is the real, finalised company voice document — not a personal writing style. It is distinct from any individual's own `voice-principles.md` (e.g. Thomas's personal email/writing style) — company brand voice governs official site copy, EDMs, social, and published content; personal voice governs one person's own drafts and emails. Don't conflate the two.

## Our personality: Courageous Ally

Our brand personality is expressed as the **Courageous Ally**. People like Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez and Adam Goodes embody it — steadfast in their vision, standing by your side. When writing for MB, ask: does this sound like something they'd say? Are you showing authenticity, empathy, and grit?

> We understand that a legal matter can feel overwhelming. We see you and stick beside you every step of the way. Listening to you and respecting what you really want. Supporting you and speaking out for what's right. Bringing tenacity to your corner. We believe in a positive vision for your future, and tirelessly strive to make it a reality.

## Why tone of voice matters

Everything we say, written or spoken, influences how people view and understand us. At every touchpoint we need to: make clients feel supported, make colleagues feel respected, make legal/adjacent professionals feel confident in our strength and expertise. Used correctly and consistently, our tone of voice helps those we want to help and attracts like-minded people to work with us.

## The three principles: Authenticity, Empathy, Grit

**Authenticity** — Our language is honest, our tone is genuine.
- We foster an environment that encourages individuality and authenticity. This helps us see each client beyond their legal matter.
- We speak from the heart. Our language is honest and our tone is genuine. We strive to understand every individual by speaking to them on their level.
- We don't skirt around difficult topics. We set a tone of openness and understanding so everybody feels safe to share their experience. Our clients seek reliability and we offer this through steadfast authenticity.

**Empathy** — Our language is accessible, our tone is warm.
- We care about the work we deliver, our clients and our colleagues. We express our expertise through a calm and reassuring tone. We use warm and direct language to make things easier to understand, because it's our job to lighten the load of our clients.
- We are people-focused, so our messaging is benefit-led. We are not a "one-size-fits-all" firm. We know that everybody is unique and that everybody's matter is different. We're here to listen, even if it means staying on a call a little longer.

**Grit** — Our language is confident, our tone is courageous.
- We are down to earth and never elitist. We are resilient for our clients and tenacious in our fight.
- We show that we are experts in our field without saying it. Our language isn't dressed up in spin; it's accessible, clear and definite. Our tone is determined and fiercely loyal.
- Grit doesn't mean we're rough or reckless, but that we're courageous; we're always ready to roll up our sleeves and do whatever it takes to find a way forward.

## Our audience

We organise our audiences into two categories and ask "is the person I'm speaking to familiar with the law or not?" — then let that guide how we communicate.

**Audience 01 — Familiar with the law**: other law firms, MB colleagues, policy makers/politicians, referrers, prospective employees, journalists, unions. Deliver clear and detailed information with authority and confidence. Speak to lawyers/adjacent professionals on their level, use terms you're both familiar with, be direct and detail-oriented.

**Audience 02 — Not familiar with the law**: individuals/businesses making a claim, people looking for class action info, people seeking guidance for a friend/family member, people new to the Australian legal system, general public. Be patient, speak with clarity, provide steadfast guidance and support. Remember each client is more than just their legal matter, set a tone that prompts openness (be more casual), use language that makes them feel comfortable and at ease.

How each principle flexes by audience — full detail, but the shape is: **familiar-with-law** gets more direct/technical/confident language; **not-familiar-with-law** gets softer, simpler, more reassuring language, with grit expressed as "don't sugarcoat things" rather than assertive expertise-signalling.

## Writing tips — dos and don'ts

**Do:**
- Choose simple words over complex ones where possible.
- Use positive language — say what something is, not what it isn't.
- Write in first or second person (I, we, you) — say "we" instead of "Maurice Blackburn."
- Read your writing aloud and ask "does this sound overly formal, wordy, or impersonal?"

**Don't:**
- Don't say "our client," "the plaintiff," or "the claimant" — use the client's name.
- Don't use abbreviations or acronyms for audiences who might be unfamiliar with them.
- Don't use jargon, clichés, or technical language.
- Don't use archaic, elaborate language.

## Worked examples (from the real guide)

**About Us page rewrite** — moved from founder-centric, achievement-focused copy ("Maurice Blackburn was born from one man's belief...") to client-first, collective "we" framing ("We stand up for you when you need us most"), with credentials/history moved lower as *evidence* rather than the opening pitch.

**Job ad rewrite** — added direct address ("you," "your"), an emotional hook ("fearlessly forward, together"), and reframed benefits/requirements around what the *candidate* gets, not just what the firm needs.

**Facebook/social post rewrite** — never write a caption purely by lifting language from a linked article. Lead with something positive/uplifting rather than a distressing opening line. Make MB's own involvement clear but secondary to the person's story ("Together, Ms Johnson and her lawyer..."). Include a Trigger/Content Warning (TW:) at the top when the linked content covers sexual abuse, suicide, or similarly distressing subject matter — this is an explicit MB house rule, not a generic social-media nicety.

**Brochureware/response-centre scripts** — soften hard "can't help" language into "unfortunately, this isn't something we can help with." Speak in first person to show empathy ("I'm worried that it might be hard for you to prove..." rather than "we are concerned that..."). Put negative information at the end of a sentence, not the start, to ease the listener in.

## Writing mechanics (from the Appendix — these are real MB house-style rules)

- **Australian English**, Macquarie Dictionary spelling (colour not color, -ise not -ize).
- **Inclusive language**: "they" as a generic pronoun rather than he/she; chair not chairman; police officer not policeman; people not mankind; disability not handicap.
- **Avoid foreign words/legalese-turned-plain-English**: "about" not "vis-à-vis," "on the face of it" not "prima facie," and a long list of legal-Latin-to-plain-English swaps (in relation to → for; in the event that → if; utilisation → use; shall have the right to → can/may, etc).
- **Numbers**: spell out one to nine, numerals from 10 up. Comma in four-digit-plus numbers (1,000). Numbers must be spelled out if they start a sentence.
- **Em dash — permitted and used deliberately** in MB's own house style, to separate a phrase/clause or mark a pause in speech ("It was brilliant — in a rash and dangerous way — and it worked"). This differs from some individuals' personal writing rules (e.g. Thomas's own `voice-principles.md` bans em dashes entirely) — that's a personal preference, not an MB brand-wide rule. Don't assume the two always agree.
- **Contractions are fine** (it's, aren't, I'll) — used deliberately to keep an informal, warm tone.
- **Client naming**: use the client's actual name, not "our client"/"the plaintiff"/"the claimant."
- **Citations/legislation**: documentary-note (endnote) system, not footnotes; Australian Guide to Legal Citation (2010, 3rd ed) for case law; "s"/"ss" for section/sections; italicise legislation titles on first full citation only.
